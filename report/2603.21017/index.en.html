1. Quick overview of the paper

One-sentence summary: This paper proposes Dream Diffusion Policy (DDP), which jointly trains the diffusion policy and diffusion world model through a shared 3D encoder, and uses real-imagination discrepancy to trigger the internal latent imagination when OOD visual disturbance occurs, thereby bypassing the contaminated visual stream and completing local recovery.

Difficulty rating: ★★★★☆. To understand the main thread, you need to be familiar with diffusion policy, 3D point-cloud policy, latent world model, OOD detection, robot closed-loop control and real system delay constraints.

Keywords: Diffusion Policy, World Model, Out-of-Distribution Robustness, Predictive Imagination, 3D Visuomotor Control.

Core contribution list

  • Robust inference via predictive imagination. Use $\mathcal{D}_{R-I}$ to detect the deviation between real observations and imagined trajectories during reasoning, triggering tracking, autoregressive latent imagination and target-specific recovery.

2. Motivation

2.1 What problem should be solved?

The paper focuses on the scenario where the robot manipulation policy encounters visual states outside the training distribution during deployment. Typical examples include the target object being suddenly pushed outside the training area, the camera screen being blocked, or the environment changing dynamically in the middle of the task. For diffusion policies that rely on visual flow, this type of input will cause the policy to continue to receive incorrect or unseen visual evidence, causing action errors to gradually amplify.

The authors frame the problem as "visual dependency vulnerability under severe OOD". Ordinary diffusion policy can be robust to minor disturbances through receding-horizon action chunking and random sampling, but the paper emphasizes that severe object displacement and complete visual occlusion will cause the model to lose reliable spatial positioning basis.

2.2 Limitations of existing methods

The paper lists several types of mitigation methods in the Introduction and Related Works: domain randomization, test-time adaptation, synthetic spatial perturbation data amplification, large pretrained visual model, enhanced representational/physical priors, inverse recovery policy and RL fine-tuning. The author pointed out that the common problems of these methods are: they either rely on continuously available visual streams, or introduce complex online pipelines, or significantly change expert actions during adaptation, thus deviating from the high-fidelity expert skill that imitation learning wants to retain.

There are existing world models or diffusion world models that can predict future states, but in the author's position, they are usually used as weakly coupled auxiliary modules, and there is no mechanism to dynamically replace the corrupted visual stream during reasoning. Therefore, the world model cannot just do passive regularization, but needs to become an active predictive intuition that can provide latent context when the sensor fails.

2.3 The solution ideas of this article

The high-level idea of DDP is: during training, let the policy representation have the ability to predict the future; during inference, if the real vision becomes untrustworthy, temporarily "close your eyes", use the latent predicted autoregressively by the world model as the policy condition, continue to generate action chunks, and restore the physical environment back to the ID state at the appropriate subtask boundary.

4. Detailed explanation of method

4.1 Method overview

The training inputs of DDP are point clouds, proprioceptions and action sequences in expert demonstrations. Shared 3D encoder $E_\psi$ extracts latent $O_{0\dots M-1}$ from historical observation $X_{0\dots M-1}$. On the one hand, these latents serve as the global condition of the diffusion policy $\pi_\theta$ to help denoise the action horizon; on the other hand, they train the diffusion world model $\mathcal{W}_\phi$ together with the future latent target to predict the future latent of the $N$ step.

When reasoning, the system starts from the ID real observation; if the difference between the world model's prediction of the current latent and the real encoder latent is too large, it is considered that the vision has entered OOD. The system then tracks the moved objects, uses predicted latent instead of corrupted real latent to condition the policy, and continues to generate future latent in a sliding window style using the world model until target-related recovery is triggered.

Figure 2. DDP closed-loop reasoning: switching between ID physical reality and OOD internal imagination; each subtask includes detection, tracking, imagination execution, and partial recovery.

4.2 Method evolution

Diffusion Policy / DP3 → DDP: DP3 has used 3D point cloud encoder to support action chunk denoising; DDP retains this action policy body, but uses the encoder for future latent prediction at the same time, so that representation learning carries dynamics regularization.

Diffusion World Model → DDP: The original diffusion world model is more biased towards the prediction or planning module; DDP is changed to feature-level integration, which repeats the historical latent to the horizon and concatenates it with the noisy future latent channel-wise to avoid relying solely on prefix-fixing conditions.

OOD adaptation/recovery → DDP: Existing methods often deal with OOD through online adaptation, amplification or recovery strategies; the change of DDP is to merge "when vision is untrustworthy" and "how to continue to control when vision is untrustworthy" into real-imagination discrepancy and latent imagination.

4.3 Core design and mathematical derivation

Action and latent chunking

The paper uses two time scales to connect the policy and the world model: the policy predicts the actions of the complete horizon $H$, but only executes the chunk length $N$ each time; the world model predicts $N$ future latents on the same chunk, and uses the last $M$ predicted latents as the next round of historical conditions in the continuous OOD rollout.

symbolmeaning
$M$Historical observation steps; both paper simulation and real robot use $M=2$.
$H$Policy is a denoise action horizon; the paper is set to $H=16$.
$N$The chunk length predicted by each actual execution and world model; the paper is set to $N=8$.

Diffusion policy loss

This formula trains the policy to recover noise from a noisy action sequence; the condition is that the historical observations extracted by the shared encoder are latent.

$$\mathcal{L}_{BC}=\mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{X}, \epsilon_p, k_p}\left[\left\|\epsilon_p-\pi_\theta\left(\mathbf{a}^{k_p}_{0\dots H-1}, k_p, \mathrm{FiLM}(\mathbf{O}_{0\dots M-1})\right)\right\|_2^2\right]$$
$\mathbf{a}_{0\dots H-1}$Expert action horizon, length $H$.
$k_p$policy diffusion timestep, randomly sampled from $\mathcal{U}(1, K)$.
$\epsilon_p$Gaussian noise added to the action.
$\mathbf{O}_{0\dots M-1}$Historical latent obtained by $E_\psi(X_{0\dots M-1})$; injected into 1D Conditional U-Net via FiLM.

Intuitively, this is still standard DDPM noise matching. The key change in DDP is not in the action loss itself, but in $\mathbf{O}$ from the shared encoder; this encoder is also constrained by the future prediction task of the world model.

Diffusion world model conditioning

This formula illustrates how the world model can provide the historical latent fixedly for each future time step, rather than only conditional on the sample prefix.

$$\mathbf{X}_{in}=\mathrm{Repeat}(\mathbf{x}_{obs}, N)\oplus\mathbf{X}_{sample}$$
$\mathbf{x}_{obs}$Historical observations latent can be understood as the structural context of the world model.
$\mathrm{Repeat}(\mathbf{x}_{obs}, N)$Copy the historical conditions to $N$ future locations, allowing each layer of 1D U-Net to access the historical structure.
$\mathbf{X}_{sample}$Noised future latent samples.
$\oplus$channel-wise concatenation.

The author believes that observation is a high-dimensional point cloud geometry latent, and action is a directional control. The two attributes are different; therefore, integrating historical latent directly at the feature level is more suitable for maintaining persistent grounding than prefix-fixing.

World model loss is symmetrical to policy loss: instead of recovering action noise, it recovers noise on future observation latents.

$$\mathcal{L}_{WM}=\mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{O}, \mathbf{a}, \epsilon_w, k_w}\left[\left\|\epsilon_w-\mathcal{W}_\phi\left(\mathbf{C}_{wm}, k_w, \mathrm{FiLM}(\mathbf{a}_{M-1\dots M+N-2})\right)\right\|_2^2\right]$$
$\mathcal{W}_\phi$Diffusion World Model, using independent but same parameter scale 1D U-Net.
$\mathbf{C}_{wm}$World model input obtained by combining historical latent and noisy future latent.
$\mathbf{a}_{M-1\dots M+N-2}$Action sequence aligned to future latent, via FiLM as global condition.

joint training objectives

The total loss puts imitation learning's action noise matching and future latent prediction in the same end-to-end update.

$$\mathcal{L}_{total}=\mathcal{L}_{BC}+\lambda\mathcal{L}_{WM}$$
$\lambda$World model loss weight; both paper simulation and real robot are set to 1.
$(\psi, \theta, \phi)$The encoder, policy and world model parameters are shared and updated simultaneously via gradient descent.
EMAThe authors use Exponential Moving Average for model weights to stabilize training and inference.

OOD detection and recovery

If the actual observed latent is too far from the predicted latent of the world model, the system will judge the current visual stream as untrustworthy.

$$\mathcal{D}_{R-I}(t)=\left\|\mathbf{O}_{real}^{t}-\mathbf{O}_{pred}^{t}\right\|_2^2$$
$\mathbf{O}_{real}^{t}$The latent of the current real observation encoded by $E_\psi$.
$\mathbf{O}_{pred}^{t}$The current latent predicted by the world model based on the previous latent and the previous action.
$\tau_{diff}$OOD threshold; in real robots, Stack Blocks is 0.0002, Pour Tea is 0.0004, and Press Button is 0.0012.

After detecting which target object has moved, DDP does not try to relearn online, but uses the pose difference as a tracking and recovery action.

$$\Delta\mathbf{p}_{C_j}=\mathbf{p}_{C_j}-\mathbf{p}_{C_j}^{exp}, \qquad \mathbf{a}^{track}_j=\Delta\mathbf{p}_{C_j}, \qquad \mathbf{a}^{rec}_j=-\Delta\mathbf{p}_{C_j}$$
$C_j$The target object of the current subtask $S_j$.
$\mathbf{p}_{C_j}$The current resting pose of the object obtained by the 6D pose estimator.
$\mathbf{p}_{C_j}^{exp}$The desired pose of the object in the ID state.

There is a clear assumption here: the moved object will eventually stabilize in a static posture, and the system only needs to estimate a single static spatial displacement, rather than continuously tracking in chaotic motion.

DDP inference pseudocode integrated version 
Input: E_psi, pi_theta, W_phi, pose estimator, subtasks S, expected poses p_exp, threshold tau_diff
OOD_Mode = False
C_disp = empty set
O_curr = E_psi(X_real^0)

for subtask S_j targeting object C_j:
  while T_end(S_j) is False:
    O_real = E_psi(X_real^t)

    if not OOD_Mode:
      O_pred = W_phi(O_real^{t-1}, a_{t-1})
      D_RI = ||O_real - O_pred||_2^2
      if D_RI > tau_diff:
        OOD_Mode = True
        C_disp = identify displaced objects by pose estimator
        if C_j in C_disp:
          execute a_track = p_Cj - p_Cj_exp
        O_curr = O_pred
      else:
        O_curr = O_real

    a_t = pi_theta(. | O_curr)
    execute action chunk a_t
    if OOD_Mode:
      O_curr = W_phi(O_curr, a_t)

  if OOD_Mode and C_j in C_disp:
    execute recovery action a_rec = -Delta p_Cj
    remove C_j from C_disp
    re-evaluate D_RI
    if D_RI <= tau_diff:
      OOD_Mode = False

Source: Text Inference Stage and Appendix "Pseudocode for Closed-Loop Inference". Note that the recovery action line in the source code pseudocode is written as $\mathbf{p}_{C_j}-\mathbf{p}_{C_j}^{exp}$, and the text definition is $\mathbf{a}^{rec}_j=-\Delta\mathbf{p}_{C_j}$; this report is interpreted according to the mathematical definition of the text.

5. Experiment

5.1 Experimental setup

Simulation benchmark

The paper uses 10 MetaWorld tasks to test general manipulation and OOD spatial shift robustness, and uses 3 Adroit tasks to test world model predictive stability in high-degree-of-freedom continuous control. The training data is 50 demonstrations for each task; all models are trained for 500 epochs and 3 seeds (0, 1, 2). During evaluation, each task and each seed were run for 100 episodes, and the average success rate was reported.

5.3 Ablation experiment

Tracking ablation. The author adds the same tracking intervention to DP3 and FlowPolicy. In MetaWorld OOD, DP3 tracking is only 23.9%, FlowPolicy tracking is only 5.4%, and DDP is 73.8%. In real robots, DP3 tracking ablation is 3.3% and DDP is 83.3%. This ablation directly corresponds to the question of "is tracking itself sufficient?"

Imagination intervention. The author randomly replaces real observations with imagined latent in the ID setting, with proportions of 10%, 50%, and 100%. MetaWorld maintains 92.3%/89.4% at 10%/50% and 61.4% at open-loop; Adroit maintains 95.2%/94.0%/93.2% at 10%/50%/open-loop respectively. This shows that the latent generated by the world model can be used as a stable alternative condition for some tasks.

Visual occlusion stress test.In the real robot, the author covers the camera with his hand to create an obvious $\mathcal{D}_{R-I}$. DP3 baseline fails, while DDP gets the job done with autoregressive imagination on Stack Blocks and Pour Tea.

6. Result analysis and discussion

6.1 Interpretation of the results given in the paper

The author's explanation of the MetaWorld results is that severe OOD spatial displacement makes base policies nearly ineffective; tracking-only baselines are still insufficient because they fail to maintain the prediction latent of action generation under corrupted observation. DDP's coupled latents enable the robot to perform actions using imagined latents during the OOD phase and return the ID visual stream after the target is restored.

Regarding imagination intervention, the author believes that DDP's world model not only fills the visual gap, but also acts as a temporal smoother. Especially in Adroit high-degree-of-freedom control, 10% imagined substitution actually increases the average success rate. The paper explains this as the stabilizing effect of imagined representation on continuous control.

For real robots, the author takes the $\mathcal{D}_{R-I}$ curve as evidence of OOD detection: a sudden displacement of the object will cause the real latent to deviate significantly from the imagined projection, and trigger the OOD mode after exceeding the threshold. In the qualitative diagram, DDP tracks and recovers object-by-object in Stack Blocks, continues to imagine pouring tea on the displaced cup in Pour Tea, and presses to the appropriate position in Press Button.

6.2 The future direction clearly stated in the paper

In Conclusion, the author proposed that in the future, meta-reinforcement learning can be used to online adapt fine-tune world model, lifelong learning can be used to retain and combine prior knowledge, or low-level tactile feedback can be added to improve the robustness of real deployment. The authors also plan to expand to more complex multi-object manipulations and explore combinations with language-conditioned methods or LLM high-level inference planning.

7. Analysis, Limitations and Boundaries

7.1 The most valuable part of this paper

Judging from the paper's own problem setting and experimental chain, the core value of DDP is to advance the world model from "auxiliary prediction/regular terms during training" to "latent conditions that can actively replace corrupted observation during inference." This enables OOD robustness not to rely solely on larger training distributions or online adaptation, but rather through real-imagination discrepancy in explicitly deciding when to abandon real vision and when to use an internal latent forecast.

7.2 Why the results hold up

The evidence chain of the paper contains three levels of comparison. First, the ID results show that DDP does not significantly harm base policy performance. Second, tracking-augmented DP3 and FlowPolicy show that "knowing where the object moved" is not enough in itself to restore success, and DDP's world model imagination is the source of the difference. Third, imagination intervention and open-loop stress test directly test whether imagined latent can be used as a policy condition; real robot experiments supplement physical deployment evidence with $\mathcal{D}_{R-I}$ curves, visual occlusion and failure-mode diagrams.

7.3 Limitations of the author's statement

  • Depends on external tracking module.DDP is highly dependent on external tracking/pose estimation such as FoundationPose++; tracking drift may accumulate in multi-stage subtasks.
  • The task must start from the ID state.The initial observation needs to be strictly in the In-Distribution region to anchor subsequent real-imagination comparison and imagined rollout.
  • Intractable composite OOD or low-level action perturbations.When OOD events are continuously superimposed, or low-level action disruptions such as gripper slippage occur, the current reasoning mechanism will become difficult.

7.4 Applicable boundaries

DDP is suitable for manipulation settings where the task can be decomposed into object-related subtasks, the target object can be positioned through a 6D pose estimator, and the OOD mainly manifests itself as spatial displacement or visual occlusion. The real system currently operates at a 2 Hz control frequency, and the authors explicitly placed the design goal on accurate recovery of severe OOD, rather than high-speed dynamic manipulation.

The simulation OOD results need to be understood according to the author's experimental settings: MetaWorld uses oracle timing, oracle state transition and MuJoCo backend operations to isolate imagination capabilities; this cannot be directly equivalent to a real tracking scenario without oracle. The real robot part supplements the verification of the detection/tracking pipeline during deployment.
